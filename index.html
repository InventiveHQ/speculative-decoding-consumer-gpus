12 findings from running local LLMs on consumer hardware

NVIDIA publishes its inference wins on 8× DGX B300s. Almost nobody has those. So we ran the same ideas — speculative decoding, quantization, offload, flash attention, power limits — on a single consumer RTX 5060 Ti (plus a 2017 GTX 1080 Ti and a CPU), and measured what actually happens. Every result below is reproducible, and several of these tricks backfire on consumer hardware — which is exactly the part the vendor blogs skip.

The test rig

Reference test rig. Every “reference” number on this page was measured on one machine — an Alienware Aurora R7:
  • CPU — Intel Core i7-8700 (6 cores / 12 threads, Coffee Lake)
  • Motherboard — Alienware 0VDT73, Intel 300-series — PCIe 3.0 root complex
  • RAM — 64 GB DDR4-2400 (4 × 16 GB, dual-channel)
  • GPU — NVIDIA RTX 5060 Ti 16 GB (Blackwell), running at PCIe 3.0 ×8 · GTX 1080 Ti 11 GB (Pascal) on the multi-GPU experiments
  • Storage — models on a SATA SSD volume (Samsung 860 EVO M.2 + SanDisk) — SATA, not NVMe
  • OS — Windows Server 2025 (build 26100)
In-VRAM throughput is GPU-memory-bandwidth bound, so bus / RAM / SSD speed barely move it — but they shape the offload and CPU results. Running this yourself? scripts/make_submission.py auto-captures your rig (CPU, RAM, GPU + PCIe link, board, storage) into the submission.

The 12 experiments

Experiment 01 · Speculative decoding

NVIDIA's 15× trick, on a $570 GPU

0.27× → 1.85×

Speculation has overhead: it backfired on the fast 7B (CUDA 0.27×) but paid off on a slow 14B (1.85× on math). And the right runtime flips by card — use Vulkan on Blackwell, CUDA on Pascal.

Experiment 03 · GGUF quantization

Where quality falls off a cliff

cliff below Q4

Output fidelity vs Q8 drops 80% (Q6) → 57% (Q4) → 41% (Q3) → 21% (Q2). The knee is ~Q4–Q5: most of the size/speed savings, output still close to full precision.

Experiment 04 · KV-cache quantization

The q4_0 cache cliff

8% similar

q8_0 KV cache is a fine space/quality trade, but q4_0 KV wrecked the output — only 8% similar to f16. The flag won't warn you; your text just quietly changes.

Experiment 05 · The VRAM cliff

When your model doesn't fit

2.9 → 43 tok/s

Sweeping a 14B's GPU layers: full-CPU is ~15× slower than full-GPU, and the climb is non-linear — the last layers matter most. Partial offload is a cliff, not a slope.

Experiment 06 · Context-length tax

What 2K→32K really costs

+1.7 GB, ~0 speed

Declaring a big context isn't free — but the cost is VRAM (+1.7 GB) and a little TTFT, not generation speed, which stays flat at ~80 tok/s until you actually fill it.

Experiment 07 · Flash attention

Is -fa actually free?

a no-op

Toggling -fa changed nothing — identical tok/s and VRAM at every context length. The reason: it's already on by default in current llama.cpp. Don't cargo-cult the flag.

Experiment 08 · Tokens per watt

Cap the power, keep the speed

−17% W, +16% eff

Capping the GPU from 180 W to its 150 W floor cost ~0 tok/s (81→80) while improving efficiency ~16%. Decoding is bandwidth-bound, so the extra watts buy almost nothing.

Experiment 09 · CPU thread scaling

More threads made it slower

peak at 6 cores

Throughput climbs to the 6 physical cores then plateaus — and 12 threads is slightly slower than 8. The "use all logical cores" default is not optimal.

Experiment 10 · Draft-model size

Bigger draft = faster? No.

0.5B wins (1.37×)

A bigger draft accepts far more (26% → 87%) but costs more per step. Net: the smallest 0.5B draft wins despite the lowest acceptance. There's a sweet spot, and it's small.

Experiment 11 · Smallest model that reasons

How small can you go?

1 → 6 of 10

Graded reasoning pass-rate climbs with size (sub-3B basically can't), becoming useful only at 7B (4/10) and best at 14B (6/10) — while speed falls 381 → 43 tok/s.

Experiment 12 · MoE on CPU

13B-class answers at 3B speed

10.6 tok/s · 8/10

A 30B-A3B Mixture-of-Experts runs at a dense 3B's speed on an 8-year-old i7-8700 — because only ~3.3B params activate per token — yet scores 8/10 vs the 3B's 1/10. ~9× faster than a dense 30B would be, for free.

The throughline

Three things show up again and again. One: single-user decoding is memory-bandwidth-bound, which is why extra watts, extra threads, and bigger context buy little speed, why an 8-year-old card isn't as far behind as its spec sheet suggests, and why a 30B MoE that reads only ~3B of weights per token runs at 3B speed on a plain CPU. Two: the datacenter tricks have overhead that only pays off in the right regime — speculation and big drafts help slow/large models, not fast small ones. Three: the convenient defaults (q4 KV, an aggressive quant, "all the threads", a heavier draft, the popular wrapper) are often the wrong call, and they fail quietly. Measure your own setup.
